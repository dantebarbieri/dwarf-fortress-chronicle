# The Year We Struck Earth

## *Being the First Annual Ledger Review of Luregold, Set Down by Atir Dumatturel, Bookkeeper*

---

*I begin this ledger at the close of Year 100, with a cup of plump helmet wine and the knowledge that seven dwarves walked into the wilderness this spring and all seven are still breathing. That alone would be worth recording. What follows is worth a second cup.*

---

## The Spring We Struck Earth

*Year 100, 1st Granite*

The seven of us — whom I have already described at length in the preamble to this chronicle, and whom I will not describe again because the ink is expensive and my patience is not — arrived at the site in early spring. The world was on fire. Twenty-five wars had erupted across the continent, Bookbalded's ashes were barely cool, and someone at Craftpractice had decided that *now* was the ideal moment to send seven people into the wild to start carving.

We brought one pick. One dream. And, I suspect, a collective failure of judgment that would have been diagnosed as madness in any civilization with better medical records.

Solon struck the first stone. He didn't say anything. He rarely does. The rest of us hauled logs, cleared brush, and began the work of turning a patch of unremarkable earth into something that might, with effort and luck and a great deal of plump helmet wine, become a home.

The initial construction was unglamorous. A stairway down. Storage rooms. A farm plot — *my* farm plot, I should note, the first seeds in the first soil, and I will maintain until my dying day that agriculture is what separates a fortress from a hole in the ground. Shorast hauled. Moldath hauled. Dastot, who was barely twenty years old and had no business being on a frontier expedition, hauled with the rest of us and didn't complain. I liked her for that.

And then there was the matter of Zefon and Ingish.

### Something Happened Over the Stone Stockpile

I should have seen it coming. The seven of us were carving out that first stairway — tight quarters, bad air, everyone stepping on everyone else's feet — and something shifted between Zefon Furpicks and Ingish Blockadeconfused. One day they were fellow founders. The next day, Zefon was carrying stones to whichever room Ingish happened to be in, and Ingish was finding reasons to be in whichever room Zefon was carrying stones to.

He says he "learned to value harmony." She says she "became more cheerful." I say they were making eyes at each other over the stone stockpile while the rest of us were hauling logs like decent, focused dwarves. I was *right there*. I saw the whole thing. Zefon would pick up a stone, glance at Ingish, and forget where he was taking it. Ingish would glance at Zefon, smile — *Ingish does not smile easily* — and then bark an order at Shorast about the wood stockpile as if nothing had happened.

Zefon felt "ambivalent" about the new romance. That is a direct quote from his own account. *Ambivalent.* The man was smitten to the point of forgetting which end of the stockpile was which, and he described his feelings as *ambivalent*. Ingish felt fond. That tells you everything about who leads and who follows in that arrangement.

They are not married. I suspect Ingish is too busy organizing the fortress to organize a wedding, and Zefon is too busy staring into the middle distance and thinking about harmony. And I am too busy having opinions about it, which I will continue to record in this ledger whether anyone asks me to or not.

*(A note in the margin, added later: they are still not married. It has been a full year. I am losing patience.)*

### My First Friend

I should record, for the sake of completeness, that I made my first friend at Luregold this spring. It was Ingish, I think — she laughed at something I said about the soil quality. I don't remember the exact remark. Something about the clay being stubborn, or the loam being disappointing, or possibly both. She laughed, and I thought: *well, at least someone here appreciates agricultural commentary.*

It was a small moment. But this is a small fortress, and small moments are what we have.

---

## What the First Autumn Brought

*Year 100, Autumn*

Five new dwarves arrived with the autumn leaves, and the fortress doubled in size overnight — or near enough. Seven became twelve. The stairway felt less empty. The dining hall felt less like a dining hall and more like a room where people actually gathered.

**Ingiz Romancetrades** came first through the gate, or near enough that he seemed first. Born in Year 38 — sixty-two years old, which made him the oldest dwarf in Luregold by a comfortable margin. An old dwarf with old hands and a steady way about him. He didn't say much about why he'd come. Old dwarves rarely explain themselves. They simply arrive.

*(He would later be seized by spirits and produce an artifact stone coffer in the grip of a possession, but that is a matter for next year's ledger. I mention it only because the look in his eyes when he arrived — that careful, searching look — makes more sense in retrospect.)*

**Unib Townveiled** walked through our gate at ninety-four years of age. Ninety-four. She has been alive since Year 6. She has a large family — children, grandchildren, a sprawling web of relations scattered across Clinching settlements. She has, by all accounts, close friends numbering exactly none.

My first impression of Unib was that she looked at our fortress the way a stonemason looks at a rough block — not with admiration, but with assessment. She noted the stockpiles. She noted the corridors. She noted, I believe, the absence of goblets, because within a week she was annoyed about having to drink without one. She was annoyed about the fish stockpile within a fortnight.

I did not know then that she would become our Mayor. I did not know that we would spend most evenings in the dining hall, arguing about policy and drinking in what I can only describe as companionable hostility. At this point, she was simply a very old woman with opinions about fish storage and a look that could curdle plump helmet wine at ten paces.

*(I was, on paper, a "passing acquaintance." This is technically accurate. We passed each other in the corridors and I acquainted myself with whatever complaint she was carrying. It was the beginning of something. I will not call it friendship. Dwarves don't call things like that.)*

And then there was young **Datan Ringdrunken**. Ten years old. A *child*, sent to the frontier. I don't know who at Craftpractice decided that a ten-year-old belonged on an expedition to an unfinished fortress in the middle of a world war, but whoever they are, I have opinions about their judgment that I will keep out of the ledger on account of the language involved.

Datan is here now. Datan is ours. That's the end of it.

Two others arrived in this wave whose names I recorded but whose stories had not yet begun. Every fortress needs its quiet settlers — the ones who show up, haul stone, eat in the dining hall, and don't cause problems. They are the backbone of the thing. I note them with respect, if not at length.

---

## The Late Autumn Wave

*Year 100, Late Autumn*

Six more. The fortress was beginning to feel *inhabited* — not just occupied, but genuinely alive. Eighteen souls now, counting the child.

**Sibrek Hamecrowded** arrived quietly, which is how Sibrek does everything. A woodcrafter by trade. Skilled hands, thoughtful manner, the sort of dwarf who prefers intellectual discussions to idle chatter and performances to parties. If you'd told me then that this gentle, poetry-loving woodcrafter would one day kill a goblin with his bare hands, I would have laughed until I choked on my wine. But that is a story for a later year, and I will not spoil it.

*(I will say only this: pay attention to the quiet ones.)*

**Ushrir Beardedspears** arrived in the same wave, and I confess I paid her no special attention at the time. She was a bone carver. We had bone carvers. Ingish was already turning out fine work. Ushrir sat down at a workshop, picked up her tools, and began. She did not introduce herself with any particular fanfare. She did not announce her intentions.

She would prove to be something extraordinary, but I did not know that yet. None of us did.

**Tekkud Atticspark** also came in this wave — born in Year 40, which made him the oldest male settler after Ingiz. A steady presence. The kind of dwarf who makes a fortress feel solid just by being in it.

The others in this group settled in with the quiet competence that I have come to associate with the best migrants. No fuss. No drama. Just dwarves finding workshops, finding beds, finding their place.

---

## The Dwarf Who Chose to Stay

*Year 100, Winter*

The last arrival of our founding year was **Avuz Somberboulder**, and her story is unlike the others. She was not assigned. She was not sent. She was, as far as I can piece together, passing through — a visitor who looked around, assessed the half-finished corridors and the cramped dining hall and the seven-turned-eighteen dwarves drinking in it, and thought: *this looks good.*

She chose to stay. Just one dwarf, arriving alone in winter, with no fanfare and no explanation beyond the obvious: that Luregold, for all its rough edges, was a place worth staying in.

I liked that. I liked it enough to note it in the ledger with an exclamation point, which I do not do lightly.

Population at the close of winter: nineteen. Seven founders, five in the first wave, six in the second, and one who simply decided this was home.

## On the Matter of Humans and Their Wars

*Year 100 — Compiled from migrant reports and caravan gossip*

I must now record something that came to us not through observation but through the mouths of migrants and merchants — secondhand accounts, thirdhand rumors, and the particular brand of fearful speculation that arrives at a frontier fortress along with the trade goods and the bad news.

**The Tall Empires have declared war upon the Guilds of Clinching.**

The war is called the Searing Conflict, and it began in this, our founding year. The humans struck first. Why? I have heard as many theories as I have shared cups of wine with new arrivals, which is to say: a great many.

Some say a trade dispute — caravans turned away, tariffs imposed, the usual commercial pettiness that civilizations use as an excuse when they've already decided to fight. Some say territorial ambition — the Tall Empires pressing outward into land they have no business claiming. I suspect something simpler and sadder: the humans are *desperate*. The Tall Empires are a dying power. The autumn merchants muttered that they number roughly ninety souls — perhaps fewer, perhaps a handful more, but the point stands. Roughly ninety humans, beset on all sides, picking fights with everyone within reach.

They are not merely fighting us. The caravans tell us that the Tall Empires also launched the War of Punches against the Slitted Scourge — the very goblins who destroyed Bookbalded — in this same year. And they have been locked in the Strifeful Conflict with the Spidery Entrances, a breakaway human faction, for some thirty-odd years now. Three wars at once. Four, if you count whatever skirmishes the elves are bringing to their borders.

A civilization of ninety souls fighting on every front. It is, depending on your temperament, either admirable or suicidal. I lean toward the latter.

### What Has Already Happened

The war did not wait for Luregold to finish its entrance ramp. Battles were fought at other Clinching sites this year — the migrants brought the names with them, muttered over dinner as though speaking them too loudly might summon the next attack.

**Pantrags** was hit. Four dwarves killed. I do not have details — the accounts are secondhand, carried by dwarves who heard it from dwarves who heard it from someone who survived. Four dead. That is what I know.

**Bookbalded** was attacked again — but Bookbalded is always being attacked. The goblins hold it, the humans assault it, the ruins change hands and change nothing. The dead at Bookbalded are beyond my counting. I have recorded what I can in the preamble to this chronicle and I will not belabor it here, except to note that even in its fallen state, Bookbalded continues to bleed.

Luregold itself has not been attacked. Not a single human soldier has appeared at our gates. Not a single arrow has been loosed in our direction. Whether this is because the Tall Empires do not yet know we exist, or because they have larger problems, or because the gods have decided to spare us this particular misery for one year — I do not know. I only know that the ledger reads *zero hostile engagements* for Year 100, and I intend to enjoy that number while it lasts.

*(It will not last. I am not a fool. But tonight, at least, the entry is clean.)*

## The Ledger at Year's End

*Year 100, Late Winter — The Bookkeeper's Annual Reckoning*

I have reviewed the ledger. The numbers are as follows, and they are exact, because I keep them and I keep them well.

**Population:** Nineteen dwarves. Seven founders who arrived in spring. Five in the first autumn wave. Six in the late autumn wave. One in winter who chose to stay of her own volition.

**Deaths:** Zero.

I want to dwell on that number. Zero. Not one. In a year when the Guilds of Clinching lost dwarves at Pantrags, when Bookbalded continued its slow bleed, when the Tall Empires declared war and the world added another conflict to a continent already drowning in them — Luregold lost nobody. Every dwarf who walked through our gate in Year 100 was still breathing when the year turned. Every founder, every migrant, every ten-year-old child and ninety-four-year-old woman and quiet bone carver and lovesick stone artist.

Zero. The number is sacred. The ledger is clean.

**Masterworks:** A handful. Ushrir and Ingish have both begun producing quality work, but the great flood of masterpieces lies ahead — I can feel it in the way Ushrir looks at her workshop, the way she picks up a bone and turns it in her fingers as though listening to it. She has not yet become what she will become. But she is starting.

**Artifacts:** None. The spirits beneath Luregold have not yet stirred. I note this without complaint. The spirits will come when they come. They always do.

**Hostile engagements within Luregold:** None. No goblins. No humans. No kobolds. No beasts from the deep. The walls are unbloodied. The corridors are quiet. The only thing that has died in this fortress is a barrel of plump helmet wine, and I take full responsibility for that.

**Trade:** Our first dwarven caravan arrived from the Guilds of Clinching. Sensible goods. Sensible traders. Sensible transactions. I had nothing to complain about, which was itself cause for complaint — a bookkeeper without grievances is a bookkeeper without purpose.

### The Bookkeeper's Private Assessment

We have survived one year. This is not nothing. By Clinching standards, it borders on miraculous. Notchedgalley lasted eight years before it fell. Bookbalded endured for decades before the goblins swallowed it whole. Cavechew held through the largest battle in recorded history, but it held *bloodily*.

We held cleanly. We held quietly. We held because we were small and lucky and far enough from the front lines that the war passed over us like a storm that spends itself on other hills.

The luck will not hold. The Searing Conflict grinds on. The goblins remember Bookbalded and will remember us eventually. The deep places are quiet, which only means they are patient.

But tonight the ledger is clean. Nineteen dwarves, all breathing. Zefon is staring at Ingish across the workshop and pretending he isn't. Ingish is pretending she doesn't notice. Solon is mining somewhere below us, silent and steady as the stone he cuts. Unib is in the dining hall, annoyed about the goblets — or the lack thereof — and drinking anyway. Datan is ten years old and asleep and safe, which is more than I can say for most children in this world.

And I am here, at this desk, with the wine and the ink and the numbers that tell me we have done something worth recording.

Nineteen dwarves. Zero deaths. One year.

We struck earth, and the earth did not strike back.
