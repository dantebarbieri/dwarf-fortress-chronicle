# The Annual Review of Year 103
## *Being the Fourth Ledger of Luregold, Kept by Atir Dumatturel, Bookkeeper*

---

*Year 103 of the Age of Myth. The wine tastes different this year. Heavier. The ink does too, if ink can taste of anything. I have more to record in this ledger than all three prior years combined, and not all of it is good, and the parts that are good are complicated by the parts that are not.*

*I will try to set things down as they happened, season by season, because this is a year where the order matters. One thing led to another, and another, and by the end we were a different fortress. Some of the differences I am proud of. Some I will carry to my grave.*

---

## Early Spring — The Flood Begins

The migrants came early this year, and they came in force.

Not the cautious trickles of the first three years — a dozen here, two dozen there — but *floods*. The gates were barely thawed from winter when the first wave arrived: families with children, craftspeople with their tools, soldiers with their kit. By the end of spring, we had taken in more new settlers than the entire population of Luregold the year before.

Among the early arrivals: **Thikut Copperzeals**, a Master Bone Carver from Craftpractice — our capital, the oldest fortress of the Guilds of Clinching. He came with his wife **Mebzuth Abbeymatches** and their children **Kadol Coalsteel** and **Kivish Esteemedmanors**. Thikut is the son of **Fikod Quakepale** and **Ingiz Romancetrades** — Fikod, who has been at Luregold since the first autumn of Year 100, one of our earliest settlers. A family reunited. I wrote that in the ledger with something approaching warmth.

I will need to cross that warmth out later.

**Lolor Giltmesh** arrived too — a young swordsman of twenty-nine, both parents already dead, with his wife **Tholtig Backletters** and their child **Zan Tingazes**. A Skilled Brewer who had picked up the sword and come to the frontier to start fresh. His wife and child are still here. He is not. I am recording this now so I do not have to record it again later.

Mebzuth herself was remarkable: an Axeman with Adequate skill, a former member of the Guild of Leopards — *my* guild — a musician, a writer, a butcher, and a warrior. She joined the militia the same week she arrived because she believed she could keep the fortress safe. She was *my* guildmate, and I did not know her well enough, and that is a regret the ledger cannot contain.

The spring waves continued. Names upon names. Families with children too young for the frontier. Old dwarves with nowhere left to go. By the time the last spring caravan had emptied its passengers, I had lost count twice and restarted the census while drinking, which may explain the margin notes that simply read "TOO MANY" in increasingly slanted handwriting.

---

## Spring — The Humans Come to Collect

And then the Tall Empires arrived.

Not as traders. Not as diplomats. Not as neighbors asking politely. They came with **Konab Spinleaped** — a human swordswoman, twenty years old, Competent with a blade and a look in her eye that said she hadn't come to negotiate — and a force behind her, and they demanded *tribute*.

We are at war with the Tall Empires. Have been since Year 100. The Searing Conflict — thirty-one engagements across four years, most of them far from Luregold, at Pantrags and Diamondgates and Sculpturecloister where our kin have bled. But Luregold itself had never been touched. We were the youngest fortress, the furthest from the front, the one the humans hadn't bothered with yet.

They bothered now.

The alert went up. Every dwarf in the fortress — all of us, including settlers who had arrived *that week* — was mustered. The records list every soul among the defenders: Solon, Ingish, Ushrir, Sibrek, even little Nish Bootfancies, who is now six years old and should not be on a military roster. We prepared for battle. We steeled ourselves.

The humans didn't attack. They demanded. And what they demanded was an artifact.

I have written about **Brandbeguiles** — the olive wood blowgun, *Zesenir*, made by Eshtan Roomclutch in a possessed frenzy in Year 102. The blowgun menacing with spikes of water buffalo leather, about which I had written in this very ledger: *"Do not let the children play with Zesenir."*

Someone — and I will not name who, because the decision was made above my pay and below my dignity — gave Brandbeguiles to the humans. The ledger says it was offered *"as a symbol of everlasting peace."*

Everlasting peace. With a civilization we are *actively at war with*. I do not know who wrote that phrase, but I suspect it was someone more concerned with surviving the afternoon than with historical accuracy. The artifact was given. Konab Spinleaped took it and left. No one died.

I crossed Brandbeguiles out of the artifact register with a single line. Not because I wanted to forget it — the Bookkeeper does not forget — but because crossing it out was the only violence available to me in that moment.

And then the fortress changed.

---

## Late Spring into Summer — The Arming of Luregold

The loss of Brandbeguiles broke something in us. Not morale — something harder than morale. Something that had let us believe, for three years, that clever crafting and smooth corridors and a thousand masterworks would be enough. That beauty would protect us. That the wars raging across the continent would stay *out there*.

A twenty-year-old human swordswoman walked through our gates and took an artifact from our hands, and we let her, because we had nothing to stop her with except wooden training swords and poets who'd been drafted into a militia they'd never asked for.

The day after the humans left, the militia commanders began reorganizing. I could hear the arguments from the farm plots. Unib was cautious — she always is, it is her one reliable trait — but the hawks had the floor and they had the fury. No more tribute. No more stolen artifacts. Not ever again.

Real steel came out of the forges. Real armor was fitted. The old wooden training weapons — blunt swords, practice axes with edges you could butter bread with, spears that were essentially pointy sticks — were pulled from the barracks and piled in a corner. Nobody wanted them anymore. The dwarves who had carried them last week looked at the new iron blades in their hands and stood a little straighter.

Training schedules were drawn up. Squads were reorganized. The drilling intensified until I could hear the clang of practice combat from the farm plots at all hours, which was annoying, but less annoying than the sound of a human swordswoman walking out of your fortress with your blowgun.

I watched the training from the balcony one evening — one of those rare quiet moments between census counts and stock ledger updates. The militia was larger than I'd ever seen it: dozens of dwarves in formation, some of them still holding their practice stances like craftspeople measuring a dovetail joint, because that's what they are. Craftspeople learning a second, more violent trade.

Zutthan Sackfrills was there — the dwarf who killed the first goblin in Year 101 with a single strike. He's a proper soldier now. So is Kikrost Busttax. So is Sakzul Walkedquake. So are dozens of others whose names I know from workshop rosters and who now also appear on military ones.

I thought: *We are sending these people to die.* And then I thought: *No. We are sending them to keep the rest of us alive.* And I did not know which thought was worse.

The fortress hummed with a new urgency. Corridors that had been quiet now echoed with boots. The barracks expanded. The forges ran hot. Weapon racks that had gathered dust were suddenly full. Every conversation, every meal, every evening at the dining hall included the same undercurrent: *the humans came and took what they wanted, and next time, next time, next time.*

It was into this frenzy that the elves arrived.

---

## Summer — The Second Insult to the Elves

The **Great Tresses** sent another caravan to Luregold. After what Unib did to them last year — the *mountain of shell crafts*, the animal products, the wordless departure, the wagon wheels scraping the corridor walls — they came back. I interpreted this as either extraordinary forgiveness or extraordinary foolishness. The elves fight seven wars simultaneously and still find time for commerce. I almost admired them.

But the fortress was not thinking about elves. The fortress was thinking about steel and training schedules and the humiliation of spring. Squads drilled in the corridors. Weapon shipments were being catalogued. Half the population had militia duties on some rotation or another, and the other half was crafting equipment for them.

Unib Townveiled — ninety-seven years old now, Proficient Appraiser, Mayor, Broker, and apparently allergic to diplomatic competence — was assigned to handle the negotiations. She was distracted. Everyone was distracted. An entire fortress had pivoted to a war footing in the span of weeks, and the elven caravan had the misfortune of arriving in the middle of it.

I was nearby, updating the stockpile ledger. I should have said something. I did not. I was busy, like everyone else, with the military inventories.

Unib surveyed the trade goods. The workshops were running at full capacity on armor and weapons. The usual stockpile of masterwork bone crafts was depleted — everything was being redirected to military supply chains. What was available? What was surplus? What could she throw at the elves to get them out of the depot so she could get back to the requisition forms?

Her eyes fell on the pile of old wooden training weapons in the corner. The ones that had been replaced by real steel. The blunt swords. The dull axes. The pointy sticks. Surplus. Useless. Taking up space.

She offered the elves a pile of old wooden training weapons.

Wooden. *Wood.* Dead trees, shaped into instruments of violence, offered to a people who consider the cutting of living wood a moral abomination. Last year it was animal products. This year it was wood. If there is a third act, I assume Unib will simply set their wagons on fire and cut out the intermediary steps.

The elves did not stare this time. They did not look at each other. They did not give Unib the courtesy of surprise. They simply turned around, packed their goods, and left. Faster than last year. More efficiently. As if they had rehearsed the departure.

I was already writing in the ledger when Unib sat down across from me.

"They'll be back," she said.

"You said that last year," I said.

"We didn't need them anyway. We needed the depot space for the new crossbow bolts."

"Unib. You offered them *wooden swords*."

She drank her wine. She did not look ashamed. Unib Townveiled has never looked ashamed in nearly a century of living, and I doubt she'll start now.

"We didn't need them anymore," she said. "We have real ones now."

This is technically true. It is also technically an international incident. I entered in the ledger: *"Elven diplomatic incident — RECURRING. Cause: distraction due to military reorganization and general fortress chaos. Unib — UNSUPERVISED."*

She read over my shoulder and laughed. I underlined "UNSUPERVISED" three times.

---

## Summer — Thieves in the Chaos

The kobolds, predictably, chose this moment of maximum distraction to creep back.

Several incursions across the summer — shadowy things at the edge of the torchlight, hands reaching for what isn't theirs. They came more boldly than before, perhaps because the fortress was in upheaval and the patrols were disorganized by the constant reshuffling of military rosters, or perhaps because kobolds are too stupid to recognize that a fortress *doubling its militia* is a worse target, not a better one.

They were chased off each time. The expanded patrols — thank the gods we had expanded patrols — caught most of them before they reached the stockpiles. One particularly brazen thief made it all the way to the food stores before being spotted by a sentry who had been on duty for exactly forty minutes of a shift that someone *finally* bothered to schedule properly.

Nobody died. Nothing of value was taken. The thieves retreated into the dark, and I filed the incident reports with the weariness of a man who has been filling out kobold-related paperwork for four consecutive years and suspects he will be doing so for forty more.

Unib still wants cage traps. I still want a moat. We still have neither, because this year we spent the budget on swords.

---

## Summer — The Moods and the Making

Amidst the chaos of military preparations, the deep places spoke twice.

The first mood seized **Thikut Copperzeals** — the new arrival, the Master Bone Carver who had walked through the gates weeks before with his wife and children and aging mother and a look of cautious hope on his face. The fey mood is different from the possessions we've seen before. A possessed dwarf is a puppet. A fey dwarf is a *conduit*. They know what they're doing. They feel the ecstasy of it. They cannot stop, and they do not want to.

Thikut danced around the workshop — literally danced, or so the crafters tell me — singing in a voice that was his and wasn't, pulling bones from the stockpile with a speed that unnerved even Ushrir. He produced **Hauntsfragrance** — *Sutonkikrost* — a bone carving of such quality that the other crafters stopped working to stare. Artifact number six.

He remembered all of it. Every cut. Every curve. He sat afterward with the expression of a man who has been somewhere very far away and is not sure he wanted to come back. Then he went back to carving — a hundred and twenty-three masterpieces before the year was out, as if the frenzy had broken something open in him and the skill just kept pouring through.

The second mood was quieter. **Kadol Tombsentered**, a Master Clothier, worshipper of Alron, member of the Company of Music — a dwarf who plays wind and percussion instruments with Adequate skill and sings well enough to be noticed — entered a secretive mood. She barred the workshop door and worked for days. When she emerged, she held **Breathcrested** — *Dumastem* — a textile creation woven with a precision that shouldn't be possible with dwarven fingers, worked with the quiet obsession of a woman who knew exactly what she was doing and chose to do it anyway.

Seven artifacts of our own forging. Six still in our possession, one in the hands of a human swordswoman. The moods come faster now — six possessions and frenzies in four years. The deep places are not whispering anymore. They are *speaking*, clearly, and I do not care for what they have to say.

In the same season, a new institution was established: **The Creative Hall**, our third guild. Where the Guild of Leopards serves the farmers and the Company of Music serves the craftsdwarves, the Creative Hall serves... well, I asked three dwarves and got three answers. Its founding members include **Thikut Boltjoys**, a Proficient Woodcutter with a surprising aptitude for singing, and Fikod Quakepale, whom I recorded as a founding member and whom I will need to mark as deceased before the year is out.

---

## Autumn — The Dwarven Caravan and the War Council

The annual caravan from the Guilds of Clinching arrived as it always does, bringing goods and gossip and the comfortable routine of commerce. We traded masterwork crafts — easy currency when your fortress produces a thousand a year. The negotiations were uneventful, which after the elven debacle and the human extortion felt like a miracle.

I had a drink with the caravan master. He asked how many we were. I said over two hundred. He looked surprised. "Last we heard, you were eighty," he said.

"We grow fast," I said.

He looked at the new weapon racks lining the corridors — the ones that hadn't been there last year — and said nothing.

The caravan was barely unpacked when the war council met. I call it a war council because that's what it was, though no one used the word. The militia commanders, the mayor, a few veterans — they gathered in Unib's office and argued through an evening while I pretended to be busy with the ledger in the next room.

The proposal: offensive operations. Strike back. The Tall Empires had humiliated us and we had the steel to answer. More ambitiously — much more ambitiously — reclaim Bookbalded from the Slitted Scourge. Take the fight to the goblins who had destroyed our sister fortress and to the demon that squatted in its ruins.

Unib was cautious. Others were not. The fact that we had two hundred dwarves with real weapons gave the hawks confidence. The humiliation of Brandbeguiles gave them fury.

The Guilds of Clinching formally declared war on the Slitted Scourge. **The Roasted Conflict**, they called it — our civilization as the aggressor, marching on the goblins for the first time. We were no longer defending. We were attacking.

Three expeditions were approved. One against the Tall Empires. One to explore old ruins. One against Bookbalded.

---

## Late Autumn — The Pillage of Sculptcradled

The first strike went south to **Sculptcradled**, a hamlet of the Tall Empires. Forty of our fighters. The force included names I knew from workshop rosters and dinner tables: Ingish Boardglazes, Ilral Brassecho, Zutthan Sackfrills, Sakzul Walkedquake, Bomrek Paddlehazy, Fikod Quakepale, Zefon Furpicks, Mebzuth Abbeymatches, Medtob Patternhall, Lolor Giltmesh — and I am naming these names because some of them will not appear again in any future ledger.

The attack was decisive. The dwarves overran the hamlet. **Sakzul Walkedquake** killed the defender **Lomno Burnbeach** in close combat. Five defending squads were shattered. The hamlet was plundered — resources seized, the Work of Sides claiming the spoils.

Our first offensive victory. The ledger records it with a notation I do not normally use: *"Site plundered — Sculptcradled — Year 103."*

But the ledger also records: **Lolor Giltmesh**, age twenty-nine, struck down during the assault. The young swordsman. The brewer. The man who arrived at Luregold *this very year* with his wife Tholtig and their child Zan, both parents already dead, looking for a fresh start in the safest place he could find.

He found a sword and a march and a hamlet full of humans and a death far from home.

His wife is still here. His child is still here. I told Tholtig myself, because someone had to, and the Bookkeeper does not delegate grief. She stared at me with dry eyes and said, "He always was the brave one."

*Lolor Giltmesh. Born Year 74. Died Year 103, at Sculptcradled. Swordsman. Father. Husband. First offensive casualty of Luregold. May the stone remember him.*

---

## Late Autumn — What the Explorers Brought Back

The second expedition — the smaller one, sent to search old ruins while the main force struck Sculptcradled — returned with something unexpected.

An artifact.

**Gloveguard** — an object of masterful craft, originally created at **Craftpractice**, our capital, in Year 50. Fifty-three years ago. It was lost in Year 69 — the same decade that saw the fall of Bookbalded and the beginning of the wars that still rage across the continent. Lost for thirty-four years in a ruin that nobody bothered to search until we sent a party with sharp eyes and sharper motives.

The scouts were vague about which ruin they explored, the way scouts are always vague about things they did in the dark. I did not press. What matters is what they brought back.

The artifact is now held by **Thikut Boltjoys**, a Proficient Woodcutter and founding member of the Creative Hall. She carries it with the careful reverence of a dwarf who understands that some things are older than the fortress she lives in.

Eight objects of power under our roof — six forged here, one recovered from silence, one stolen and carried in the form of a grudge. The Bookkeeper notes that recovering artifacts from ruins is excellent work and recommends further expeditions. The Bookkeeper also notes that he would like to recover a specific olive wood blowgun from a specific human swordswoman, but that is a matter for the generals, not the ledger.

*(It is absolutely a matter for the ledger. I am putting it in the ledger. Return Brandbeguiles.)*

---

## Late Autumn — The Siege of Incinerating

*I need to write this. I have been putting it off. The wine is not helping. Nothing is helping. I am the Bookkeeper, and the Bookkeeper records what happens, even when what happens is this.*

---

Bookbalded. Our sister fortress. The first and greatest loss of the Guilds of Clinching, destroyed in Year 94 when the goblins of the Slitted Scourge and the demon Dungboil the Gall of Filths murdered a hundred and fifty of our people in a single year. I have written about Bookbalded in the intro to this chronicle, in Year 102's assessment, in the margins of every ledger page where I had space and wine enough to be angry. Bookbalded is a wound. It has never healed.

The success at Sculptcradled emboldened the hawks. We had struck the humans and won. The new war against the Slitted Scourge was declared. The largest expedition Luregold had ever assembled was gathered and sent south to Bookbalded, to demand its surrender, to reclaim what was ours.

Thirty-nine of our notable fighters marched, plus their supporting squads. Among them:

**Zefon Furpicks.** One of the original seven. *My* Zefon. The quiet stone carver who watches Ingish from across the workshop and never says what he's thinking and loves her without ever having the courage or the need to put it into words. Zefon had already marched to Sculptcradled and come back unharmed. Now he was marching again, deeper, further, to a place where something worse waited.

I don't know why he volunteered. He has never shown interest in combat. He is not in the militia rosters. He is a carver. But he walked through the gates with a weapon on his back and a look on his face that I have never seen before and hope I never see again. It was the look of a man who has decided something and will not be talked out of it.

Ingish was in the workshop when he left. I know because I was watching, because I always watch, because it's my job and my curse. She did not look up. She was carving bone — her eighty-ninth masterpiece of the year — and she did not look up, and I do not know if that was because she didn't notice or because she couldn't bear to.

Also marching: **Fikod Quakepale**, age seventy-four. Expert Bowyer. Mother and grandmother. One of Luregold's earliest settlers. A woman who should have been sitting by the hearth drinking wine with me, not marching to face a demon.

**Bomrek Paddlehazy**, age thirty. Fikod's son. A wrestler with Competent dodging and a love of music — percussion, strings, a singing voice that carried through the dining hall. His wife Erush Loverring stayed behind with their children Logem Beancrafts and Zulban Pillarblameless.

**Mebzuth Abbeymatches**, age thirty-four. Thikut Copperzeals' wife. My guildmate. She had held a position in the Work of Sides that same year. Musician, writer, butcher, soldier. She marched because she believed she could make a difference.

She was right. The difference she made will haunt me.

---

They found Bookbalded as the legends describe it: broken. Occupied. Eight notable defenders held the ruins — seven goblins and the demon.

**Dungboil the Gall of Filths.**

The thing that killed a hundred and forty of our people. The thing that turned Bookbalded from a thriving fortress into a tomb. The thing I warned about in the margins of the Year 102 ledger with underlined ink and a drawing of a skull.

I wrote then: *"If Dungboil comes to Luregold, the Bookkeeper's recommendation is: close the drawbridge, seal the gates, and pray."* What I did not anticipate was that *we* would go to *Dungboil*.

---

The dwarves fought with a fury that the legends will remember. They killed *seven of the eight defenders*. Every goblin at Bookbalded died that day:

**Ngebzo Menacerazors** — struck down by Mebzuth Abbeymatches. The same Ngebzo who had killed Fikod Quakepale moments earlier. Fikod: Mebzuth's mother-in-law. Mebzuth drove her axe into the goblin who killed her husband's mother and did not stop.

**Dang Bloatedcurses** — also killed by Mebzuth. Two kills in the chaos of a battle against a demon. A thirty-four-year-old axe-wielding musician killed two goblins in the ruins of a fortress that died before she was born.

**Anomi Closeknow** and **Ini Blushedshock** — both killed by **Minkot Tongsblots**, who cut through them like a dwarf who had something to prove and proved it twice.

**Ago Dousedclout** — killed by **Cilob Trustgranite**.

**Tastro Purgeequaled** — killed by **Oddom Boltverses**. Oddom, who would not survive the hour.

**Am Jadesieged** — killed by **Kikrost Busttax**.

Seven goblins. Seven kills. Four unnamed defenders besides. The creatures who had squatted in the bones of our sister fortress for nine years — all dead. The dwarves tore through them with a vengeance that had been building since Year 94.

And then there was Dungboil. Alone. The last defender. A demon.

---

**Fikod Quakepale** fell first. Age seventy-four. Expert Bowyer. Struck down by Ngebzo Menacerazors before the goblin was killed. She had walked through the gates of Luregold in the autumn of Year 100 and walked through the ruins of Bookbalded in the autumn of Year 103 and did not walk out.

Then Dungboil began.

**Iteb Theaterconvents**, age twenty-four. Wrestler. Adept Carpenter. Worshipper of Alron. Member of the Creative Hall. A builder asked to fight. Struck down.

**Bomrek Paddlehazy**, age thirty. Fikod's son. The musician. He died in the same ruins as his mother, to the same demon. Struck down.

**Dakost Lensearthen**, age fifty-eight. Worshipper of Alron. She had arrived at Luregold *this very year*. She had been here for months. Struck down.

**Oddom Boltverses**, age thirty. She had killed Tastro Purgeequaled — one of the seven goblins — moments before. The demon struck back. Struck down.

**Logem Treasuremines**, age twenty-two. The youngest to die. Twenty-two. She had been at Luregold since Year 102. A single year. Struck down.

**Mebzuth Abbeymatches**, age thirty-four. My guildmate. The fiercest fighter on the field by any measure: two goblin kills, including the one who killed her mother-in-law. And then the demon reached her. I am told she was still swinging when she fell. The ledger does not record this. I am recording it here. Struck down.

**Reg Craftsmatch**, age sixty-one. The only one killed not by a blow but by a shot — Dungboil used some form of ranged attack. She was sixty-one years old. She should have been carving bone in a warm workshop. Shot down.

**Muthkat Crowdedbridge**, age thirty-one. Maceman. Skilled Clothier. She played wind instruments and sang. Member of the Company of Music and the Doctrine of Gold. The last named dwarf to fall. Struck down by Dungboil. The demon's eighth kill of the day.

---

Nine dwarves. Nine of ours. Eight killed by a single demon.

The surviving dwarves retreated. They had killed every goblin at Bookbalded — all seven — and four unnamed defenders besides. They had fought with courage that I cannot find adequate words for in this ledger or any other. And they could not kill the demon.

Dungboil the Gall of Filths remains at Bookbalded. Alone now. The goblins are dead. The ruins are empty of everything except a demon and the bones of the dwarves who tried to bring it down.

---

## Early Winter — The Reckoning

Zefon came back through the gates, and I set down my pen, and I went to the dining hall, and I poured two wines, and I sat across from him. He looked at me with the hollow eyes of a man who has seen something that cannot be unseen.

"How many?" I asked, because someone had to.

"Nine," he said. He drank the wine. All of it. He set the cup down and said nothing else for a very long time.

I sat with him. It's what I do. It's all I can do.

---

The matter of Thikut Copperzeals must be recorded, because the ledger demands it and because no one else will.

In the spring of Year 103, Thikut arrived at Luregold with his wife, his children, and his aging mother. He was reunited with his father Ingiz. He entered a fey mood and created an artifact. He produced a hundred and twenty-three masterpieces. He joined the Company of Music. He was happy, or as close to happy as dwarves allow themselves to be.

In the autumn of Year 103, he lost his mother, his brother, and his wife. All in the same battle. At the same ruins. To the same demon and its goblins. His wife killed the goblin who killed his mother, and then the demon killed his wife.

Three members of his family. One battle. One demon.

Thikut did not go to Bookbalded. He stayed at Luregold. He was in the workshop when the survivors came through the gates. He was carving bone — because that is what Thikut does, that is what a Master Bone Carver does, he *carves* — and I do not know who told him, or how, or whether he stopped carving when he heard.

His children Kadol Coalsteel and Kivish Esteemedmanors, motherless now, are here. His father Ingiz Romancetrades, widowed, is here. The family that was reunited in spring was broken in autumn.

I went to find Thikut after I completed the death certificates. He was in the workshop. Carving. The bone under his hands was becoming something beautiful, because that is the only thing Thikut knows how to do with pain — turn it into something beautiful. I set a cup of wine next to him and said nothing, and he said nothing, and neither of us needed to.

Five of the dead were worshippers of Alron — five faithful of the Doctrine of Gold, killed in a single battle. Ushrir's flock of twenty-four became nineteen. She held the rites the evening the survivors returned, and I am told she wept while performing them, which I have never seen. I will not record that in the official ledger because a priest's tears are not the fortress's business. But they are this chronicle's business. She wept. She finished the rites. She went back to the workshop.

---

## Winter — The Numbers

The fortress grieves, and the fortress carves. Both are true. Both happen simultaneously. The workshops did not close for the dead. They never do.

**Population:** We stand at two hundred and twenty-two living souls. Two hundred and thirty-two walked through our gates this year, but ten will never walk back. The corridors that felt spacious for forty are suffocating for two hundred. The dining hall runs three shifts. I attend the fourth.

**The dead:** Ten dwarves, total. One at Sculptcradled, nine at Bookbalded. Lolor Giltmesh, Fikod Quakepale, Bomrek Paddlehazy, Iteb Theaterconvents, Dakost Lensearthen, Oddom Boltverses, Logem Treasuremines, Mebzuth Abbeymatches, Reg Craftsmatch, Muthkat Crowdedbridge. I have carved their names into the memorial myself. The stonemasons offered. I said no. If my letters are unsteady, it is because my hands shook.

**Artifacts:** Eight under our influence — six forged at Luregold, one recovered from a ruin, one stolen and remembered. The blowgun Brandbeguiles is in human hands. We gained Hauntsfragrance, Breathcrested, and Gloveguard. Net gain: two. The ledger does not record grudges, but the Bookkeeper does.

**Masterworks:** One thousand and twenty-three produced this year. The total since founding now stands at one thousand, eight hundred and eighty-nine. Ushrir Beardedspears leads with two hundred and forty-eight this year — her lifetime total surpassing six hundred and sixty. Rovod Cobaltfortunes matched her stride with two hundred and forty-seven. Bomrek Bandedinks added two hundred and twenty-six. Thikut Copperzeals, in his first year, produced a hundred and twenty-three plus one artifact, carving through grief as if the bone were the only thing holding him together, and perhaps it was. Ingish Blockadeconfused added eighty-nine.

**Wars:** Two. The Searing Conflict with the Tall Empires grinds on — thirty-one engagements now, and we have finally struck back. ## The Ledger Entry for Year 103, Month 12

*Written by lamplight. The fortress is not quiet tonight. There is hammering somewhere below — always hammering — and somewhere above, someone is playing a wind instrument badly, which means the music lessons are continuing, which means life is continuing, which is something.*

Luregold stands. Four winters. Two hundred and twenty-two souls. Eight artifacts. Nearly two thousand masterworks. Three guilds, one temple, two wars, one demon that we could not kill.

This is the year we lost our innocence, if frontier fortresses in the middle of a world war are entitled to innocence. We armed ourselves because we had to. We struck back because we could. We marched to Bookbalded because we believed we were ready.

We were not ready. I do not know if we will ever be ready for Dungboil. But the Bookkeeper notes, for the record, that we killed everything else at Bookbalded. Every goblin. Every unnamed defender. Everything that was not a demon. We are not weak. We are simply not yet strong enough for what waits in the dark.

Solon mines. He went deeper this year. I told him not to. He grunted. He went deeper. That's Solon.

Unib is in the dining hall. She is drinking without a goblet again. I would go sit with her but I am here, in the Bookkeeper's office, writing by lamplight, and the wine is within reach, and the ledger is open, and the ink is drying on the names of ten people who were alive this time last year and are not alive now.

Zefon is somewhere. I haven't seen him since dinner. He ate quickly and left. He does that now. Before Bookbalded, he lingered. He carved. He watched Ingish. Now he eats and leaves and I don't know where he goes, and I think he sits in the dark somewhere and tries to forget what he saw, and I think he fails, and I think he'll keep trying anyway, because that's what dwarves do. We keep trying.

And somewhere, in a workshop that smells of bone dust and plump helmet wine, a Master Bone Carver who lost everything in a single afternoon carves something beautiful out of the wreckage, and does not stop, and does not stop, and does not stop.
