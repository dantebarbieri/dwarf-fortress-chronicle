# Year 101 — The Second Annual Review of the Fortress of Luregold

*Being the ledger review of Atir Dumatturel, called Roughlearning, Bookkeeper, Grandmaster Planter, and one of the seven who struck earth.*

*Second year. Still alive. Still drinking. The ink budget has doubled. The wine budget has tripled. I consider this acceptable.*

---

## Spring, Year 101 — On the Arrivals Who Would Change Everything

The second spring found us nineteen strong and digging. Solon had the lower stairways roughed in. Ingish had the work orders humming. Zefon was carving and staring at Ingish and pretending he wasn't staring at Ingish. Normal operations.

Then the fifth wave came over the hill.

I keep a list. Wave 5 brought — among others — **Lor Whiskeredboulder**, a quiet woman born in Year 60. Unremarkable on arrival. I wrote her name in the ledger and moved on. I also recorded **Zutthan Sackfrills**, a family man with carpentry skills and a look about him like he'd left something behind he intended to protect from a distance. **Bomrek Bandedinks** walked in with the same wave — competent hands, steady temperament, the kind of dwarf you don't notice until six months later when you realize half the stockpile is his work. I noted him and moved on.

And then there was **Nish Bootfancies**.

Three years old. *Three.* A child who should have been learning to walk in a safe corridor in Craftpractice, not trudging across a war-torn continent to a frontier fortress that had existed for twelve months. I don't know who authorized this. I don't know which bureaucrat at the capital looked at a requisition form that said "settlers for Luregold" and thought, "yes, the toddler." I wrote a formal objection in the ledger margin. Nobody read it. Nobody reads the margins except me.

Nish is ours now. The fortress will raise her. I have opinions about this, and I have written them down, and the wine stain next to those opinions tells you how I felt while writing them.

The population grew. We were no longer a handful of founders scratching at stone — we were becoming something. Not a fortress, not yet. But something with weight.

## On the First Dwarf Who Lost Her Mind — Lor Whiskeredboulder and the Hatch Cover

It started with Lor.

She had been with us only a short while when she stopped speaking. Not gradually — not the slow quiet of someone who doesn't have much to say. One morning she simply *stopped*. Her eyes went somewhere else. Somewhere I could not follow and would not want to.

She walked to a craftsdwarf's workshop. She barred the door from the inside. She worked for days without food, without water, without rest. The sounds from inside were — I will say *purposeful*. Not frantic. Whatever was driving her hands knew exactly what it wanted.

She emerged holding a **hatch cover**.

*Ripedepressed the System of Sides.* In our tongue: *Ethramozon Idith Adek.*

I was in the stockpile when she walked out. I saw her face. It was the face of someone waking from a dream they cannot remember — not confused, exactly, but *empty*. As if the thing that had been inside her had taken something when it left. Or given something she didn't know how to carry.

It is, by any objective measure, the finest hatch cover in the world. I catalogued it. Its value is incalculable. It is a *hatch cover*. For covering hatches. The spirits of the deep earth seized a woman's mind and body, worked her hands like a puppet for days, and produced the world's most magnificent piece of architecture for keeping things from falling down stairs.

Lor didn't remember making it. She went back to work the next day as if nothing had happened. She later joined the **Guild of Leopards** — my guild, the farmers — which I choose to interpret as evidence of good character and sound judgment, possession notwithstanding.

I wrote in the ledger: *"Artifact created. Hatch cover. Possessed mood. Creator does not recall."* Then I poured a drink and stared at the entry for a long time.

This was the first. It would not be the last.

## Summer, Year 101 — On the Woman Who Carved and Prayed

I need to talk about Ushrir.

I recorded her arrival last year — Wave 3, autumn of Year 100. **Ushrir Beardedspears.** At the time I noted her as a competent crafter, one name among several. I noted that she mastered bone carving in our first winter, which was fast — faster than anyone had a right to be — but I had other concerns. Population reports. Stockpile inventories. Wine.

I was not paying close enough attention.

By the summer of this year, Ushrir had surpassed every crafter in the fortress. Not by a little. Not by degrees you could argue about over drinks. She had *left us behind*. She carved bone the way water runs downhill — inevitably, endlessly, without apparent effort. Masterpiece after masterpiece came from her workshop. The rest of us produce a masterwork and feel *satisfied*. Ushrir produced a masterwork and reached for the next bone.

She became **Legendary Bone Carver**. The ledger confirms it. I have the date. I stared at it.

But here is what makes Ushrir *Ushrir*: she doesn't just carve. She *prays*.

This summer she was proud — not of her craft, though she had every right to be. She was proud of a fine Door she'd made and of the establishment of a temple for the **Doctrine of Gold**. A religion. Our fortress religion, or the beginnings of one — twenty-four worshippers gathering to pray to **Alron** in the space that would become the **Sanctum of Silver**. And Ushrir at its center. The **Holy Rock** — the priest.

By autumn, she was performing the rites of the Doctrine of Gold in that dedicated temple and she felt *wonder*. I read the word in the report three times. *Wonder.* A Legendary crafter who has already surpassed everyone in this fortress, a woman whose hands produce beauty faster than the rest of us can catalogue it, and she still feels *wonder* at the act of worship. As if the craft is not the point. As if the craft is just the thing her hands do while her soul is busy with something else entirely.

I am not a religious dwarf. I follow Alron because most of us do, the way one follows the current in a river — not out of conviction but because it's easier than swimming against it. But when I watch Ushrir in the Sanctum of Silver, hands still dusty from the workshop, eyes closed, I think — perhaps there are dwarves for whom the gods are not distant. Perhaps some of us are simply *closer*.

I have catalogued her output. She has already surpassed everyone in the fortress, and the year is not yet over. I do not know where her ceiling is. I am not certain she has one.

## On the Second Dwarf Who Lost His Mind — Dodok Whipwhispers and the Mussel Shell Earrings

The sixth wave arrived in the autumn, and among them came **Dodok Whipwhispers**. I recorded his name. I recorded his skills. Within weeks, the spirits took him too.

Same blank stare. Same locked workshop. Same sounds of hands working with a purpose that did not belong to the dwarf attached to them.

He emerged holding a set of **mussel shell earrings**.

*The Yawning Hermit. Amoshosh.*

Mussel shell earrings. Named "The Yawning Hermit." I sat with this fact for the better part of an evening and a bottle.

I don't know what the spirits were trying to tell us. I don't know why the most profound supernatural forces in the deep earth — the same forces that have touched this fortress twice now — chose to manifest as *jewelry made from shellfish*. The same mussel shells our fisherdwarves produce by the cartload. The same mussel shells that sit in rotting piles near the butcher's shop, waiting to be hauled away or carved into trinkets or stepped on by distracted miners on their way to the stairwell.

I catalogued them. Their value is extraordinary. They are earrings. Made of shellfish.

The spirits have a sense of humor. I have noted this in the margin. The spirits also have *terrible taste in materials*, and I have noted that too.

## Autumn, Year 101 — The Autumn Zutthan Drew Blood

I was in the stockpile — cataloguing, as usual — when the alarm went up.

**Stosbub Cruelcolumn.** A goblin of the **Slitted Scourge**. Born in Year -57. One hundred and fifty-eight years old. A creature that had been alive since before the world had civilizations, before the first dwarven pick struck stone, before any of us were even a possibility. A member of the same warband that *destroyed Bookbalded* and killed a hundred and fifty of our kin in a single year.

He came to Luregold. Perhaps he was a scout. Perhaps he was a remnant, still prowling the trade roads, still hungry after all those years. Perhaps he remembered what happened at Bookbalded — the way the walls fell, the way the corridors filled with the dead — and he wanted to do it again.

He found **Zutthan Sackfrills**.

Zutthan is a member of the **Brains of Crafting** — our militia squad, a melee unit that wears metal armor in theory. In practice, we don't actually have any metal armor yet. I have noted this in the ledger fourteen times. I have underlined it twice. Nobody has read those notes either.

Zutthan is a Novice Organizer, a Dabbling Carpenter, and a father of three children. His wife is **Ilral Brassecho**. I have their names in the population register. I know their children's names. I know which dormitory they sleep in. These are the things a bookkeeper knows — the small facts that become unbearable when someone doesn't come home.

The other dwarves say Zutthan's bravery comes from defending his children. I believe it. There is a specific kind of fury that a parent brings to combat — not rage, exactly, but a *refusal*. A refusal to let the world take one more thing.

Zutthan struck Stosbub Cruelcolumn with weapon #694. A single blow. The goblin fell.

One hundred and fifty-eight years of malice, ended by a father with no metal armor defending a fortress not yet two years old.

I catalogued the kill. First blood at Luregold — ours drawn, not spilled. The Slitted Scourge destroyed Bookbalded. They killed our kin. They occupied our halls. And now one of their own lies dead on our floor, struck down by a carpenter's son who wasn't supposed to be a soldier.

I had a drink.

Zutthan went home to Ilral and the children. He didn't celebrate. He didn't tell stories in the dining hall. He just... went home. Sat down. Held his children.

That's the part they don't put in the war records. The going home. The sitting down. The quiet.

I put it in my ledger, because someone should.

## What the Autumn Caravan Told Us

The dwarven caravan arrived from Craftpractice, and the merchants brought more than trade goods. They brought news, and the news was grim.

**Diamondgates** — one of our sister fortresses, mountain halls to the north — was struck by the Tall Empires. Five dwarves dead. Five names I don't have yet, because the merchants couldn't remember them, or didn't want to. Five more for the ledger of the Guilds of Clinching, which grows longer every season.

**Sculpturecloister** was hit as well. One dwarf killed. The merchants said it was a skirmish, a probing attack, the humans testing defenses. One dwarf dead is not a skirmish to the dwarf who died.

The **Searing Conflict** grinds on. As I recorded last year, the Tall Empires declared war on us the very season we founded Luregold. Twenty-odd battles now, across half a dozen sites. Bookbalded's ruins are still being fought over — both sides clawing at the bones of a fortress that neither can properly hold. Pantrags took losses last year. Now Diamondgates and Sculpturecloister.

Luregold remains untouched.

I wrote "untouched" in the ledger and then stared at the word for a while, because "untouched" is not the same as "safe," and a bookkeeper should know the difference.

The merchants also carried a rumor from the west: the **Lilac of Perfecting** — an elven civilization that fights more wars than most goblin hordes — conquered the human town of **Frilllove** from the Tall Empires. The elves are carving the humans apart from one direction while the goblins gnaw from the other, and we hold our line in the center, and the Tall Empires shrink with every season.

My assessment, written in the margin: the Tall Empires are dying. They picked fights with everyone — us, the goblins, the elves, a breakaway human faction that's been bleeding them for thirty-six years. They are down to fewer than a hundred souls. This war will end when they cease to exist, which may be soon. I do not know if this is good news. A cornered animal fights hardest.

## On Kobolds and Other Vermin

The filthy little creatures came in the night.

The first kobold incursion of the year was a theft — some wretched thing with grabbing hands creeping through the trade corridor, fingers closing around anything shiny or small enough to carry. Chased off. No injuries on either side.

I was more annoyed than frightened. Kobolds are not goblins. Kobolds are not the Slitted Scourge, are not the destroyers of Bookbalded. They are vermin with ambition. But vermin with ambition can become vermin with *confidence*, and confidence in kobolds leads to abduction attempts, and abduction attempts lead to missing children, and I have a three-year-old in this fortress now, thanks to whoever at Craftpractice thought frontier settlement was appropriate childcare.

Nobody died. Not even a kobold, which I find mildly disappointing.

I brought the matter to Unib — she was Broker still, not yet Mayor, but she was already the dwarf everyone went to with problems. I told her we needed better defenses. I proposed a moat. She proposed cage traps. We argued about it for an hour and resolved nothing, which is how most of our conversations end — with no conclusion and less wine than we started with.

The ledger entry reads: *"Kobold incursion — theft — no casualties — defenses INADEQUATE."* I underlined the last word. Unib saw it and said nothing, which meant she agreed but would never admit it.

## On the Third Dwarf Who Lost His Mind — Ingiz Romancetrades and the Stone Box

Three.

Three possessions in a single year. Three dwarves seized by forces from the deep, their hands worked by something ancient, their minds emptied and filled and emptied again. I have catalogued all three and I do not understand any of them.

**Ingiz Romancetrades** was the oldest dwarf in the fortress when it happened. Born in Year 38 — he was sixty-three years old, with old hands and old eyes and, as it turned out, old spirits living inside them. He arrived in Wave 2, back in the autumn of Year 100. I recorded him then as a quiet elder, the kind of dwarf who carries the weight of decades in the way he walks. He worshipped his gods and kept to himself.

Then the spirits took him. Same as Lor. Same as Dodok. The blank stare. The barred workshop. The purposeful sounds.

He produced a **siltstone coffer**. *Dwelledtwilight. Kokebingtak.*

A stone box. For storing things. It is the finest stone box in the world. I have catalogued it and noted its value as "immeasurable" and its function as "storing socks, probably."

Three possessions. A hatch cover, mussel shell earrings, and a stone box. The spirits are *active* beneath Luregold. They are reaching up through the stone, through the carved corridors and the rough stairways, and they are seizing our people and using them to make objects of terrible beauty and mundane purpose. I don't know what this means. I don't know if it's a blessing — three artifacts in one year, each worth more than the entire fortress treasury — or a warning.

I have written in the margin, in small letters: *"What do the spirits want?"*

No answer has been forthcoming. I did not expect one. I had a drink instead.

## Winter, Year 101 — On the Matter of a Mayor

It happened in winter. The fortress had grown enough — enough dwarves, enough corridors, enough complexity — that someone decided we needed formal governance. An election, or the dwarven equivalent: a consensus that solidifies around whoever has been running things already and simply makes it official.

**Unib Townveiled** became Mayor of Luregold.

I should have seen it coming. She was already our Broker — had been since she arrived in Wave 2, ninety-four years old and knowing exactly what everything was worth and what everyone wanted. She handled the caravans. She heard the petitions. She knew every name in the fortress and nobody's secrets, which is the perfect qualification for a mayor: universal familiarity, zero intimacy.

Ninety-six years old now. She has been alive since Year 6. She has a large family scattered across the Guilds of Clinching, worships several deities with the methodical devotion of a woman who hedges her spiritual bets, and is classified in my records as a "passing acquaintance" of virtually every dwarf in the fortress.

Ninety-six years old and no close friends. The rumors say she's too busy keeping order — mandates to enforce, petitions to hear, the fish stockpile to complain about. She knows everyone's name and nobody's heart. She is a Talented Bone Carver, a Proficient Appraiser, and she was *annoyed* in our first summer because she had to drink without a goblet. She has opinions about goblets. She has opinions about everything. She simply doesn't share them unless the sharing is efficient.

When the appointment became official, I found her in the dining hall. She was drinking. I sat down.

"Mayor," I said.

"Bookkeeper," she said.

"Congratulations."

"Is that what this is."

I poured. She drank. I drank. This is how it started — our arrangement. Not friendship. Something more efficient than friendship. Two old dwarves — well, one old dwarf and one who merely *feels* old — sitting across from each other in the dining hall, complaining about their respective domains. She complains about mandates and petitioners. I complain about the ledger and the ink budget. Neither of us says we are glad the other one is there, because dwarves don't say things like that.

But I write it here, in the margin, where nobody reads: I am glad she is there.

My most recent thought: euphoric due to inebriation.
Her most recent thought: euphoric due to inebriation.

We understand each other.

## A Reckoning of the Second Year

*Written by lamplight. Wine within reach. The ledger open to the annual summary.*

The numbers first, because the numbers are what I am for:

Population: growing. We began this year with nineteen dwarves. Two waves arrived — Wave 5 in the spring, Wave 6 in the autumn — and the fortress swelled. I will not give an exact count here because the ledger page is wine-stained and I am not certain I can read my own handwriting, but we are north of forty souls now, and every one of them is breathing, which is the number that matters most.

Deaths: zero. Two years. Zero deaths. I write this with the care of a man who knows how quickly such a record can break. Bookbalded had zero deaths once. Notchedgalley had zero deaths once. I am not superstitious — that would be Ushrir's department — but I will not celebrate the number. I will simply record it and hope.

Artifacts: three. *Ripedepressed the System of Sides* — a hatch cover by Lor Whiskeredboulder. *The Yawning Hermit* — mussel shell earrings by Dodok Whipwhispers. *Dwelledtwilight* — a siltstone coffer by Ingiz Romancetrades. All possessed moods. None of them remember what they made. The spirits are beneath us, and they are busy.

Kills: one. Zutthan Sackfrills, weapon #694, versus Stosbub Cruelcolumn of the Slitted Scourge, one hundred and fifty-eight years old. The first goblin blood spilled at Luregold. I raised a glass. Zutthan went home to his family.

Religion: the Doctrine of Gold is established. The Sanctum of Silver has a priest — Ushrir Beardedspears, Holy Rock — and twenty-four faithful who gather to worship Alron. I am among them, mostly out of habit. Ushrir is among them out of something deeper.

Governance: Unib Townveiled, Mayor and Broker since this winter. She runs the fortress now, officially, which is different from running it *unofficially* only in that she can now issue mandates and be annoyed about them at the same time.

The war: still burning. Diamondgates and Sculpturecloister took losses — six dwarves dead between them. The elves are eating the Tall Empires from the west. The goblins hold Bookbalded's ruins. Luregold stands untouched, which is a gift I do not trust and cannot explain.

The ledger is clean. The numbers are good. I do not know what the third year will bring — more settlers, certainly. More possessions, possibly. The spirits seem to like us. The goblins have noticed us. The kobolds are testing us. Somewhere out there, the Slitted Scourge knows that one of their own didn't come back from Luregold.

Sibrek Hamecrowded is in the militia now, drilling with the Brains of Crafting, a thoughtful woodcrafter who loves poetry and doesn't know yet what the next year will ask of him. Ushrir is in the Sanctum, carving and praying. Bomrek is at his workshop, steady and quiet, and I have a feeling his name will appear in this ledger more often as the months wear on. And Unib is in the dining hall — I can hear her from here, complaining about the fish stockpile — and tomorrow we will sit across from each other again, and she will tell me what's wrong with the fortress, and I will tell her what's wrong with the ledger, and the wine will be passable, and neither of us will say we are friends.

This is Luregold at the end of its second year. Not yet a fortress. Not yet a legend. But something with weight, and roots, and stone walls that have held against everything the world has thrown at them so far.

The key words being *so far*.

*— Atir Dumatturel, Bookkeeper, Year 101*
